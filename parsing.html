Parsing

I donâ€™t really remember when I started to learn how to parse cryptic crossword clues; the only thing I do really remember are the places where clues started to make sense.

There are two places fundamental to my ability to do crosswords: first and foremost, the living room sofa at home, where my Mum and I would sit with tea and try to complete the Telegraph or Guardian crosswords together. Second, the bar at my college where, after early morning lectures, I would congregate with a group of non-science students who were just getting up, and we would have tea or coffee and try to do the Guardian crossword together. There were particular setters who caused a frisson of discomfort when their names were spotted, as we knew we were in for a tough time without the ease of anagrams (Bunthorne, in particular, was my nemesis).

These places and people taught me how to pull apart clues, to understand the cues and signals embedded within them, and how to translate them into other words. A little bit like trying to create this website.

There is no getting around that a lot of this requires British cultural knowledge; not just any old cultural knowledge, but a particular streak of the British fee-paying school (followed, of course, by Oxbridge) realm of knowledge embedded within it - terms for serving within the armed forces, cricket, opera, Shakespeare quotes, that sort of thing. Therefore, the below may not help a lot, but hopefully it will move the needle in the right direction.
