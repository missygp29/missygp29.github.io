Shakespeare in His Infinite Variety

A guide for teens and everyone who is William S-curious

Why Shakespeare?

Reading and knowing more about Shakespeare taps into a couple of things that are important for young adults.

First, a commitment to learning, particularly reading for pleasure.

Second, if you find a love for Shakespeare, it can open a doorway to creative activities, such as working in the theater.

So, read on, Macduff. (Sorry, corny Macbeth joke there.)

How to Read Shakespeare

Most of us come across Shakespeare for the first time in a setting not conducive to creating love for Shakespeare - in a classroom, assigned reading (very possibly an excerpt, rather than the whole thing), instead of in a performance that shows us the rhythm, the flow, the mood, the context. These plays were made to be performed, not published and read in what can be a very sterile environment.

Shakespeare isn't easy for our modern ears. If you have not been taught how to read him, it's no fun. It's arduous to look up every other word.

But, if you can get into the rhythm of his work, it can be exhilarating. There's lots that's problematic (see more below!), but so much to probe and luxuriate in. So, here are some tricks and tips to help us learn how to understand Shakespeare's words.

Movie Adaptations

There are a million and one Shakespeare adaptations - those that are more closely based on the plays, and those that put a different spin on things (and take liberties such as making something like Taming of the Shrew less utterly misogynistic.)

The Taming of the Shrew is a vicious play, and this version softens it and allows us to root thoroughly for Kat and Patrick.

This production is... not without its issues; it's worth examining race in this movie. Without ignoring that, this movie is also swoony, exciting, and captures the headiness of their love in a way that not many others manage. Also, Harold Perrineau as Mercutio is glorious.

Twelfth Night gets a reboot in this aughts classic, where Viola pretends to be a boy to play on a boys' soccer team.

If older classics and directors are more your thing, Kurosawa's adaptation of Macbeth has been called the best Shakespeare movie adaptation ever.

Book Adaptations

If you like the stories of the original plays, there are many books based on Shakespeare's works. Some are direct adaptations; others are responses to him, using his characters and creations to expand on and challenge his thinking.

Below, you'll find just a few of the myriad books out there. If you don't like the look of these, googling Shakespeare adaptations will get you lists you can work through; Book Riot is a good place to start.

In the room beneath a stage’s trapdoor, Shakespeare’s dead teenage girls compare their experiences and retell the stories of their lives, their loves, and their fates in their own words. Bestselling author Joy McCullough offers a brilliant testament to how young women can support each other and reclaim their stories in the aftermath of trauma.

Perfect for fans of The Last Magician and Descendant of the Crane, this heart-stopping debut is an imaginative Romeo and Juliet retelling set in 1920s Shanghai, with rival gangs and a monster in the depths of the Huangpu River.

In Hamnet, Maggie O'Farrell tells the imagined story of Hamnet, Shakespeare's son and namesake for Hamlet. O'Farrell focuses on Anne Hathaway, Shakespeare's wife, and the family home. It's a moving and sad work full of beautiful descriptions of being a woman in 16th century England.

A retelling of Hamlet, with Hanalee investigating what happened to her Black father, who was married to Hanalee's White mother at a time when society and the Ku Klux Klan would not accept an interracial relationship.

Shakespearean Insults

And, lastly but possibly most importantly: Want to take your insulting to another level? Stand out from the crowd? Shakespeare is famous for his insults! No matter what you're trying to say, it's likely that Shakespeare has an insult for you.
