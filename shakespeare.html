Shakespeare in His Infinite Variety

A guide for teens and everyone who is William S-curious

Why Shakespeare?

Reading and knowing more about Shakespeare taps into a couple of things that are important for young adults.

First, a commitment to learning, particularly reading for pleasure.

Second, if you find a love for Shakespeare, it can open a doorway to creative activities, such as working in the theater or poetry.

How to Read Shakespeare

Most of us come across Shakespeare for the first time in a setting in a classroom with it as assigned reading (an excerpt, not even the whole thing). It's a miracle any of us grow to love his work. These plays were made to be performed.

Shakespeare isn't easy for our modern ears. And there is a lot that's problematic, but so much to enjoy. Here are some tricks and tips to understand Shakespeare's words.

What to Read

So, you're going to read some Shakespeare. There are so many versions! People swear by the Arden versions, but they can be intimidating (so. many. notes.). If you can get Pelican versions, go for it! (You should be able to get some from your library or secondhand online at places like Thrift Books or Abe Books, for example); Folger has free versions! But which plays? And how? Here are a few ways you could approach it:

  • Select plays for which you can find good filmed versions - whether films of theater performances, movies that adhere faithfully to the original play, or updates. Watch it first and try to find the rhythm and feel, with the printed version to read along and make notes, if that helps.
  • Read aloud a play with friends! It might feel weird to ask them to do it, but a read aloud is likely to get the feel of it. And you can assist each other in understanding.
  • Find a buddy to read all the plays! You can get a collected works and read them all individually and compare. The Storygraph has a great readalong with friends feature. You could go by reading them in the order in your book, if you have the collected works. My (Pelican) copy has them alphabetically within genre, and I wanted to mix it up a bit and see the progression as Shakespeare aged and grew. So, I'm using the Wikipedia version and I'll stick to that as I reread them.

  • And for a really big challenge, some people read them all in a year! It takes about 15-20 minutes of reading per day, allegedly. If that seems intriguing, check out this Folger article about it and the schedule.
  • Lastly, you could bypass the plays altogether and read the poems!

Movie Adaptations

There are a million and one Shakespeare adaptations - those that are more closely based on the plays, and those that put a different spin on things (and take liberties such as making something like Taming of the Shrew less utterly misogynistic.)

The Taming of the Shrew is a vicious play, and this version softens it and allows us to root thoroughly for Kat and Patrick.

This production is... not without its issues; it's worth examining race in this movie. Without ignoring that, this movie is also swoony, exciting, and captures the headiness of their love in a way that not many others manage. Also, Harold Perrineau as Mercutio is glorious.

Twelfth Night gets a reboot in this aughts classic, where Viola pretends to be a boy to play on a boys' soccer team.

If older classics and directors are more your thing, Kurosawa's adaptation of Macbeth has been called the best Shakespeare movie adaptation ever. Kurosawa also made a version of King Lear, called Ran.

This is a gory, disturbing, and incredibly designed adaptation (as you'd expect from the designer of The Lion King on Broadway) of an under-performed play about violence and revenge.

Book Adaptations

If you like the stories of the original plays, there are many books based on Shakespeare's works. Some are direct adaptations; others are responses to him, using his characters and creations to expand on and challenge his thinking.

Below, you'll find just a few of the myriad books out there. If you don't like the look of these, googling Shakespeare adaptations will get you lists you can work through; Book Riot is a good place to start.

[ VERSE NOVEL] In the room beneath a stage’s trapdoor, Shakespeare’s dead teenage girls compare their experiences and retell the stories of their lives, their loves, and their fates in their own words. Bestselling author Joy McCullough offers a brilliant testament to how young women can support each other and reclaim their stories in the aftermath of trauma.

Perfect for fans of The Last Magician and Descendant of the Crane, this heart-stopping debut is an imaginative Romeo and Juliet retelling set in 1920s Shanghai, with rival gangs and a monster in the depths of the Huangpu River.

In Hamnet, Maggie O'Farrell tells the imagined story of Hamnet, Shakespeare's son and namesake for Hamlet. O'Farrell focuses on Anne Hathaway, Shakespeare's wife, and the family home. It's a moving and sad work full of beautiful descriptions of being a woman in 16th century England.

A retelling of Hamlet, with Hanalee investigating what happened to her Black father, who was married to Hanalee's White mother at a time when society and the Ku Klux Klan would not accept an interracial relationship.

We use a huge number of phrases today that were first recorded or originated in Shakespeare's plays. Seriously, huge. Ever heard the phrase "star cross'd lovers"? Romeo and Juliet. "In stitches"? Twelfth Night. "Break the ice"? Taming of the Shrew. Here are a couple of more comprehensive lists.

Criticism

Shakespeare has been studied and analyzed for centuries. In the past few decades, in particular, scholars have looked at what his works tell us about race and gender.

Shakespearean Insults

And, lastly but possibly most importantly: Want to take your insulting to another level? Stand out from the crowd? Shakespeare is famous for his insults! No matter what you're trying to say, it's likely that Shakespeare has an insult for you.
